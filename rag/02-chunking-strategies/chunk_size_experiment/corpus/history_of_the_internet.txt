The internet's origins trace back to ARPANET, a project funded by the U.S. Department of Defense's Advanced Research Projects Agency in the late 1960s. ARPANET's first successful connection between two computers, at UCLA and Stanford Research Institute, took place in 1969, and the network grew over the following years to link a small number of university and research computers together. ARPANET's key innovation was packet switching, a method of breaking data into small packets that could be routed independently across a network and reassembled at their destination, which proved far more resilient and efficient than the circuit-switching approach used by the telephone network of the era.

As ARPANET grew, researchers recognized the need for a standardized way for different, independently operated networks to communicate with one another. Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn developed the Transmission Control Protocol and Internet Protocol, together known as TCP/IP, throughout the 1970s, and ARPANET formally switched over to TCP/IP on January 1, 1983, a date often cited as the technical birth of the modern internet. TCP/IP's design as an open, non-proprietary standard that any network could adopt is a large part of why the internet was able to grow into a single, interconnected global network rather than fragmenting into incompatible regional systems.

Throughout the 1980s, the network expanded well beyond its military and academic research origins. The U.S. National Science Foundation built NSFNET, a backbone network connecting supercomputing centers and university networks across the country, which for a time served as the central backbone of the rapidly growing internet in the United States. Domain Name System, or DNS, was introduced in 1983, replacing the unwieldy practice of maintaining a single master text file mapping every computer name to its numerical address with a distributed, hierarchical naming system that could scale to a much larger network — the same basic system, mapping human-readable domain names to numerical IP addresses, is still used today.

The World Wide Web, which many people conflate with the internet itself, is actually just one application built on top of the internet's underlying network infrastructure. Tim Berners-Lee, a researcher at CERN, the European particle physics laboratory, proposed the Web in 1989 as a way for researchers to share and link documents easily, and he built the first web browser and web server in 1990 and 1991. The Web's core innovations were HTML, a simple markup language for formatting documents and embedding clickable hyperlinks; HTTP, a protocol for requesting and transmitting those documents; and URLs, a standardized way to address any document anywhere on the network. Crucially, Berners-Lee and CERN made the Web's underlying technology freely available to anyone, with no licensing fees, which was instrumental in its rapid global adoption throughout the 1990s.

The early-to-mid 1990s saw the internet transition from an academic and government network into a public, commercial medium. The Mosaic browser, released in 1993, was the first to display images inline with text rather than in separate windows, making the Web dramatically more visually appealing and accessible to non-technical users, and it directly inspired Netscape Navigator, which became the dominant browser of the mid-1990s. Internet service providers began offering dial-up access to ordinary consumers, and by the second half of the decade, millions of households were connecting to the internet over standard telephone lines, typically at speeds measured in mere tens of kilobits per second.

The rapid commercial growth of the internet during the 1990s fueled the dot-com boom, a period of intense investor enthusiasm for internet-based companies, many of which had little or no profit and sometimes little revenue at all, but were valued on the promise of future growth in an entirely new market. Companies like Amazon and eBay, founded in the mid-1990s, represented genuinely durable new business models built around the internet's unique capabilities. But a large number of speculative startups with weak underlying business fundamentals also raised enormous sums of money during this period, and when investor sentiment shifted in 2000, the resulting crash, often called the dot-com bust, wiped out a huge amount of paper wealth and bankrupted many companies, though the surviving companies and the underlying infrastructure investment laid important groundwork for the internet's next phase of growth.

Broadband internet access, including cable and DSL connections, became widely available to consumers during the early 2000s, offering speeds far higher than dial-up and, critically, an "always-on" connection that didn't tie up a household's phone line. This shift fundamentally changed what kinds of applications were practical to build: streaming audio and video, large file downloads, and always-connected applications all became viable for ordinary consumers in a way they simply hadn't been over a dial-up modem. Broadband adoption is widely seen as one of the key enabling factors behind the social media and online video platforms that emerged later in the decade.

Social media platforms transformed the internet from primarily a network of static documents into a network centered on user-generated content and social connections. Friendster and MySpace pioneered mainstream social networking in the early-to-mid 2000s, followed by Facebook, which launched in 2004 initially as a network restricted to college students before opening to the general public in 2006 and eventually growing into one of the largest platforms in the world. Twitter, launched in 2006, popularized short, real-time public posts as a new form of online communication. YouTube, also launched in 2005, made video the dominant content format on the Web for huge segments of the internet's users, a shift that traditional broadcast media had to adapt to.

The launch of the iPhone in 2007 and the wave of smartphones that followed fundamentally reshaped how people accessed the internet. For the first time, a fast, capable internet connection was something most people carried in their pocket at all times, rather than something accessed only from a desktop computer at home or work. Mobile apps became a major new software distribution model alongside the traditional Web, and mobile internet traffic eventually surpassed desktop traffic globally. This shift also accelerated the rise of location-based services, mobile payments, and on-demand services like ride-hailing apps that simply weren't possible without an always-connected mobile device.

Cloud computing emerged as a major shift in how internet infrastructure itself is built and operated. Rather than companies buying and maintaining their own physical servers, cloud providers like Amazon Web Services, launched in 2006, Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud rent computing power, storage, and a wide range of managed services over the internet, billed on a flexible, pay-as-you-go basis. This dramatically lowered the upfront cost and technical complexity of launching an internet-scale service, since a small startup could rent the same underlying infrastructure as a large enterprise rather than building a data center from scratch, which is part of why the pace of new internet services accelerated significantly after cloud computing became mainstream.

The technical architecture connecting all of this together relies on a small number of foundational, mostly invisible systems. The Border Gateway Protocol, or BGP, allows the thousands of independently operated networks that make up the internet to exchange routing information about how to reach each other, effectively making the internet a "network of networks" rather than a single centrally managed system. Content delivery networks cache and serve content from servers physically close to end users around the world, reducing the latency and bandwidth cost of delivering high-traffic content like video and large web pages. Internet exchange points are physical facilities where many different networks connect directly to each other, improving performance and reducing cost compared to routing traffic through a third party.

As the internet became central to commerce, communication, and daily life, security became an increasingly urgent concern. Early internet protocols were largely designed without strong security in mind, since the original network connected a small number of trusted research institutions. As the network opened to the general public, encryption protocols like SSL and its successor TLS became essential for protecting sensitive data such as passwords and payment information in transit, and the padlock icon in a browser's address bar, indicating an encrypted HTTPS connection, became a now-familiar signal to users that a site is taking basic security precautions. Cybersecurity threats evolved alongside the internet's growth, from early computer viruses to large-scale data breaches, ransomware, and state-sponsored attacks on critical infrastructure.

Internet governance — the question of who sets technical standards and policy for a global network with no single owner — has remained a genuinely difficult and evolving problem. Technical standards bodies like the Internet Engineering Task Force develop and maintain the core protocols the internet runs on through an open, consensus-driven process. The Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers, or ICANN, coordinates the domain name system and IP address allocation globally. Beyond pure technical coordination, individual governments increasingly regulate areas like data privacy, content moderation, antitrust enforcement against large platforms, and national security, creating an evolving and sometimes contentious patchwork of rules that apply differently depending on a user's location, even though the underlying network itself remains fundamentally global.

Today's internet looks dramatically different from the small research network of the 1970s, but its core technical foundations — packet switching, TCP/IP, and DNS — remain largely unchanged, a testament to how well those original designs scaled. What has changed is everything built on top: a handful of research computers has grown into billions of connected devices; static text documents have given way to video, social media, and interactive applications; and an academic curiosity has become critical global infrastructure underpinning commerce, communication, and access to information for most of the world's population.

Before search engines matured, finding information on the Web meant browsing hand-curated directories of links, a model pioneered by early services like Yahoo. Google, founded in 1998, displaced these directories by ranking pages with its PageRank algorithm, which treated a link from one page to another as a vote of confidence and weighted those votes by the importance of the linking page itself, producing dramatically more relevant search results than competitors at the time. Search quickly became the internet's primary entry point for most users, and the advertising business model built around search results, charging advertisers for clicks rather than impressions, became one of the most lucrative business models in the history of the medium.

The open source software movement grew up alongside the internet and became deeply intertwined with its infrastructure. Software like the Linux operating system, the Apache web server, and countless programming languages and libraries are developed collaboratively and distributed freely, with their source code openly available for anyone to inspect, modify, and redistribute. A large share of the servers that power the internet today run on open source software, and the collaborative development model pioneered by open source projects, with public code repositories, distributed contributors, and transparent decision-making, has itself become a significant influence on how software is built well beyond the open source world specifically.

Net neutrality is the principle that internet service providers should treat all data traveling over their networks equally, without charging different prices, slowing down, or blocking specific websites, applications, or services. Proponents argue that net neutrality has been essential to the internet's open, permissionless character, allowing a small startup's traffic to reach users exactly as reliably as a large established company's traffic. Opponents argue that internet service providers should have more flexibility to manage network traffic and offer differentiated service tiers. The debate has played out repeatedly in regulatory and legal battles in the United States and other countries, with policy shifting back and forth as different regulatory bodies and administrations have taken different positions on the issue.

The most recent phase of the internet's evolution involves connecting far more than just computers and phones. The Internet of Things refers to the growing number of physical devices — thermostats, doorbells, industrial sensors, and countless other everyday objects — that are now connected to the internet, collecting and exchanging data. Fifth-generation, or 5G, mobile networks were designed in part to support this growth, offering substantially higher bandwidth and lower latency than earlier mobile generations, which matters for applications like autonomous vehicles and industrial automation that depend on near-instantaneous, reliable connectivity rather than just raw download speed.

Despite the internet's enormous reach, access to it remains far from universal, a gap commonly referred to as the digital divide. Significant portions of the world's population, particularly in rural areas and lower-income countries, still lack affordable, reliable internet access, which increasingly excludes them from education, employment, government services, and information that are taken for granted elsewhere. Even within wealthy countries, gaps in internet access and digital literacy persist along economic, geographic, and generational lines, and closing this divide remains an active focus of both government policy and private investment, including satellite internet constellations designed to bring connectivity to areas that traditional cable and cellular infrastructure don't economically reach.
