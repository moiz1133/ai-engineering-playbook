The greenhouse effect is the physical mechanism that keeps Earth's surface warm enough to support life, and it is also the mechanism behind human-caused climate change. Sunlight passes through the atmosphere and warms the Earth's surface; the surface then radiates that energy back out as infrared heat. Certain gases in the atmosphere, called greenhouse gases, absorb some of that outgoing infrared radiation and re-emit it in all directions, including back toward the surface, which traps additional heat that would otherwise escape directly into space. Without any greenhouse effect at all, Earth's average surface temperature would be well below freezing; the issue with climate change is not that the greenhouse effect exists, but that human activity has substantially increased the concentration of greenhouse gases, intensifying the effect beyond its natural balance.

Carbon dioxide is the most important greenhouse gas in the climate change discussion, not because it traps the most heat per molecule, but because of the sheer quantity humans have added to the atmosphere, primarily by burning fossil fuels — coal, oil, and natural gas — for energy, transportation, and industry. Methane is a far more potent greenhouse gas molecule for molecule, but it persists in the atmosphere for a much shorter time before breaking down. It comes from livestock digestion, rice paddies, landfills, and leaks from oil and gas infrastructure. Nitrous oxide, largely from agricultural fertilizers, and various synthetic fluorinated gases used in refrigeration and industry round out the major contributors, each with its own atmospheric lifetime and warming potency.

The carbon cycle describes how carbon moves between the atmosphere, oceans, land, and living organisms. Under natural conditions, this cycle is roughly balanced: plants absorb carbon dioxide through photosynthesis, some of it returns to the atmosphere through respiration and decomposition, and the oceans absorb and release carbon dioxide at the surface in a slow exchange with the atmosphere. Burning fossil fuels releases carbon that had been locked away underground for millions of years, adding it to the active cycle faster than natural processes can reabsorb it. The result is a steady increase in atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration, which has risen from around 280 parts per million before industrialization to over 420 parts per million today, a level not seen in at least several hundred thousand years based on ice core records.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change, or IPCC, was established in 1988 by the United Nations to provide policymakers with regular, comprehensive scientific assessments of climate change, its impacts, and possible response strategies. The IPCC does not conduct its own original research; instead it synthesizes and evaluates the existing published climate science literature through periodic assessment reports written and reviewed by thousands of scientists from around the world. These reports have become the most authoritative reference point in international climate policy discussions, including the negotiations that produced the Paris Agreement.

Global average temperature has already risen by more than one degree Celsius above pre-industrial levels, and the rate of warming has accelerated over the past several decades. This might sound like a small number, but a one or two degree shift in global average temperature represents an enormous amount of additional energy trapped in the climate system, and its effects are not distributed evenly: some regions, particularly the Arctic, are warming two to three times faster than the global average, a phenomenon known as Arctic amplification, driven partly by the loss of reflective sea ice exposing darker ocean water that absorbs more sunlight.

Rising temperatures are causing measurable sea level rise through two main mechanisms. First, ocean water expands slightly as it warms, a process called thermal expansion, which by itself accounts for a significant share of observed sea level rise. Second, melting land ice — primarily from the Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets and from mountain glaciers worldwide — adds new water volume to the oceans that wasn't there before. Sea level rise threatens low-lying coastal cities and island nations with more frequent flooding, increased coastal erosion, and the contamination of freshwater supplies by encroaching saltwater.

Climate change is also associated with shifts in the frequency and intensity of extreme weather events. Warmer air holds more moisture, which can intensify rainfall and increase the risk of flooding during storms. Heat waves are becoming more frequent, more intense, and longer-lasting in many regions. In some areas, changing precipitation patterns and higher temperatures are increasing the severity and length of droughts and the conditions that fuel wildfires. It's important to note that climate scientists generally don't claim any single weather event was "caused" by climate change; instead, a growing field called attribution science estimates how much more likely or more severe a given event was made by the background warming trend.

Climate models are the primary scientific tool used to project how the climate system will respond to different future trajectories of greenhouse gas emissions. These models divide the atmosphere, oceans, and land surface into a three-dimensional grid and simulate the physical laws governing fluid dynamics, radiation, and energy transfer within and between each grid cell, run forward in time on supercomputers. Models are validated by checking whether they can reproduce historical climate observations, and despite running on simplified grids, the major global climate models have a strong track record of correctly projecting the broad trajectory of global warming over recent decades.

Responses to climate change are generally divided into two complementary categories: mitigation and adaptation. Mitigation refers to actions that reduce greenhouse gas emissions or remove carbon already in the atmosphere, addressing the root cause of the problem — examples include switching electricity generation from fossil fuels to renewable sources, improving energy efficiency, and reforestation. Adaptation refers to actions that help people and ecosystems cope with the climate change that is already happening or is now unavoidable, such as building sea walls, developing drought-resistant crops, and redesigning infrastructure to withstand more extreme weather. Most serious climate strategies pursue both simultaneously, since even aggressive mitigation today would not fully halt the warming already set in motion.

Renewable energy sources — primarily solar, wind, and hydroelectric power — generate electricity without directly burning fossil fuels, making them central to most mitigation strategies. The cost of solar photovoltaic panels and wind turbines has fallen dramatically over the past two decades, to the point where in many regions new renewable electricity is now cheaper than building new fossil fuel power plants. The main technical challenge with solar and wind is intermittency: they only generate power when the sun shines or the wind blows, which has driven major investment in energy storage technologies, particularly large-scale batteries, as well as in upgrading electrical grids to move power efficiently across regions with different weather conditions.

The Paris Agreement, adopted in 2015 by nearly every country in the world, is the central international framework for coordinating climate action. Unlike its predecessor, the Kyoto Protocol, the Paris Agreement does not set binding emissions targets for individual countries from above. Instead, each country submits its own "nationally determined contribution," a self-set emissions reduction target, and the agreement establishes a process for countries to report their progress transparently and to increase the ambition of their targets over time. Its central goal is to keep global warming well below two degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, with an aspirational target of limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius.

Carbon pricing is one of the most widely discussed policy mechanisms for reducing emissions because it directly targets the underlying economic incentive problem: emitting carbon dioxide is currently free for most emitters, even though it imposes real costs on society. A carbon tax sets a fixed price per ton of emitted carbon dioxide, while a cap-and-trade system sets a fixed total limit on emissions and lets companies buy and sell permits to emit within that limit, letting the market determine the price. Both approaches aim to make low-carbon alternatives more competitive by making high-carbon activities more expensive, though the political feasibility and design details vary enormously between countries and regions.

Beyond reducing new emissions, a growing area of climate research and policy focuses on carbon removal — actively pulling carbon dioxide that has already been emitted back out of the atmosphere. Natural approaches include large-scale reforestation and changes to agricultural practices that increase the amount of carbon stored in soil. Engineered approaches include direct air capture facilities that chemically extract carbon dioxide from ambient air, and various forms of carbon capture and storage that intercept emissions at industrial sources before they reach the atmosphere. Most climate scenarios that limit warming to 1.5 or 2 degrees Celsius rely on some amount of carbon removal alongside steep emissions cuts, since some sectors, like aviation and cement production, are particularly difficult to fully decarbonize with current technology.

Climate change is fundamentally a global commons problem: greenhouse gases mix throughout the atmosphere regardless of where they were emitted, so the benefits of any single country's emissions reductions are shared by the whole world, while the costs of reducing emissions are borne locally. This mismatch between local costs and global benefits is part of why international coordination through frameworks like the IPCC and the Paris Agreement has proven essential, even as individual countries continue to debate the pace and methods of their own domestic climate policies.

Roughly a quarter of the carbon dioxide humans emit is absorbed by the oceans, which has a side effect distinct from warming: ocean acidification. When carbon dioxide dissolves in seawater it forms carbonic acid, gradually lowering the ocean's pH and reducing the availability of carbonate ions that many marine organisms, including corals, oysters, and certain plankton species, need to build their shells and skeletons. This makes ocean acidification a direct chemical consequence of rising atmospheric carbon dioxide, separate from temperature change, and one with serious implications for marine food webs and the fishing and aquaculture industries that depend on them.

Climate change is also a major driver of biodiversity loss. As regional climates shift faster than many species can migrate or adapt, habitats that species depend on can disappear or change beyond recognition, and the timing mismatches that result — for example, when flowers bloom before their pollinators emerge — can disrupt ecological relationships that took thousands of years to establish. Coral reefs are an especially visible example: sustained ocean warming causes coral bleaching, where corals expel the symbiotic algae that give them both color and nutrients, and repeated or severe bleaching events can kill reefs outright, eliminating habitat for the roughly a quarter of all marine species that depend on reefs at some point in their life cycle.

The impacts of climate change are not distributed equally across the world's population, a pattern often discussed under the term climate justice. Many of the countries and communities that have contributed the least to historical greenhouse gas emissions, often in the developing world, face some of the most severe impacts, including increased exposure to drought, flooding, and extreme heat, while having the fewest financial resources to adapt. Within wealthier countries too, lower-income communities often face disproportionate exposure to climate-related risks such as urban heat and flooding, while having less capacity to relocate or rebuild after a climate-related disaster, which has made equity a central and often contentious theme in climate policy debates.

Discussions of climate solutions sometimes focus heavily on individual actions, like reducing personal car travel or air travel, while climate scientists and policy analysts generally emphasize that systemic changes — in how electricity is generated, how buildings are constructed, and how industries operate — account for the large majority of the emissions reductions needed to meet international climate targets. This doesn't mean individual choices are irrelevant, but it does mean that policy changes affecting entire energy and industrial systems are generally considered far more consequential than individual consumer behavior alone for the scale of emissions reduction required.

Geoengineering refers to large-scale, deliberate interventions in the climate system beyond conventional mitigation and adaptation, and it remains one of the most scientifically and politically contentious areas of climate response. Solar radiation management proposals, such as injecting reflective aerosol particles into the stratosphere to mimic the cooling effect of a large volcanic eruption, could in principle lower global temperatures relatively quickly and cheaply, but they don't address the underlying buildup of greenhouse gases, carry significant uncertainty about regional side effects on weather patterns, and raise difficult governance questions about who would have the authority to deploy such a technology at a planetary scale. Most climate scientists view geoengineering as, at best, a potential emergency backstop rather than a substitute for reducing emissions.
